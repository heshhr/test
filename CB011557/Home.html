Sri Lanka

Sri Lankan Turtle Population

Five of the world’s seven sea turtle species including green turtles, leatherbacks, olive ridleys, hawksbills, and loggerheads come to nest on Sri Lankan beaches. The south and southeast coastlines with vast areas of seagrass beds and coral reefs provide important nesting and foraging grounds for sea turtles. In this area human population density is high and tourism is also largely concentrated along the coasts. While fishing is the primary source of income in most Sri Lankan coastal communities, people also depend heavily on other available natural resources including sea turtles. The poverty of coastal communities is often associated with the exploitation of meat, eggs, and other products of turtles. In addition to the food that turtle meat and eggs provide for an individual’s household, there are economic benefits associated with the sale of turtle meat, eggs, and scutes in the market.

Declines in sea turtle populations are a major concern for conservation biologists and today all sea turtle species are globally protected. In Sri Lanka, under the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance (FFPO, 1938 amended in 1972) it is an offence to capture, kill, injure, or possess sea turtles or their eggs. Sri Lanka has banned the international trade of sea turtle products. Although this has resulted in a considerable decline in slaughtering, sea turtles and their eggs continue to be exploited in some parts of the country. Until the mid-1990s, the most widespread forms of sea turtle exploitation have been the collection of eggs and the killing of adults for their meat and scutes. The FFPO was amended in 1993, subsequently increasing the punishment for offenders. Although the killing of sea turtles for their scutes to produce ornaments gradually decreased after this strict law enforcement, turtle eggs are still eaten or sold by the local community in some areas. Moreover, incidental capture of sea turtles in various fisheries along the northwestern, western, and southwestern coast of Sri Lanka has been reported which is widely recognized as an important issue in the conservation and recovery of these threatened and endangered species.

Turtles Biology and Natural History

Groupings in the Animal Kingdom

The reptiles (Reptilia), are a group of cold-blooded vertebrates (Vertebrata) that are most suited to life on land, including turtles and tortoises (technically: Testudines). Sea turtles (Chelonioidea) have made a secondary return to living in the water, as has frequently happened with other groups in the evolutionary history of predominantly land-dwelling vertebrates, which also include birds and mammals.They must return to land to breed, and they need air to breathe.

The majority of species, including sea turtles, are Cryptodira, which are side-necked turtles (Pleurodira; retraction of the head into the shell by a horizontal S-shaped movement), and hidden-necked turtles (Cryptodira; retraction of the head by a vertical S-shaped movement). Land tortoises, soft-shelled tortoises, alligator tortoises, and snapping turtles are some more species of Cryptodira that are recognized. Many species, such the European pond turtle, which is also widespread in Central Europe, and the North American pond sliders, are well suited to live in water. Sea turtles, on the other hand, are the group that has continually adapted to live in water.

Turtle Life Cycle

  1. Reach sexual maturity after 10-40 years (female green sea turtles in the Western Pacific average 33 years).
  2. everal decades of reproductive activity (female green sea turtles in the western Pacific average 19 years).
  3. Excellent ability to retrieve natal site after decades of maturity.
  4. Several years between nesting seasons (remigration intervals).
  5. Multiple clutches per nesting season (internesting intervals).
  6. Unusually high reproductive rate for reptiles (r-rate; survival probability to sexual maturity about 1:1000).

Reproduction of Turtles

Mating & courting Behavior:

  • Mating takes place near the nesting beaches
  • Female mates only before first egg laying of each nesting season
  • Male courtship behavior not very subtle
  • Fierce biting fights between male

Incubation of eggs in the sand:

  • Eggs are buried in sand (0.5-1m) and incubated by its temperature
  • Proper conditions (temperature, sand texture, humidity) important for hatching success: turtles must be selective in choosing nesting site

Turtle Hatchery Sri Lanka

The turtle hatcheries of Sri Lanka are vital conservation centers that play a crucial role in protecting and preserving the country's endangered turtle species. hese hatcheries provide a safe and controlled environment for sea turtles to lay their eggs and ensure the survival of their offspring.
