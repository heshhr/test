TURTLE CATEGORIES IN SRI LANKA

Brief Introduction

We are providing you with information on sea turtles that use Sri Lanka's coastline as a nesting habitat nowadays. They are the Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea), Loggerhead (Caretta caretta), Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas), Hawksbill Turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata), and Leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea). In Tamil and Sinhala, the sea turtles are referred to as Amai and Kesbawa, respectively.

Green Turtle

Green turtles may weigh up to 230 kg and have an average length of 1 meter. All tropical and subtropical oceans, including the Indian, Atlantic, Caribbean, and Pacific Oceans, are home to this migratory fish. Green turtles are caught and slaughtered to produce the delicacy known as "turtle soup" in various cultures. Their English name alludes to the hue of the soup-making fat that may be discovered beneath their shells. The majority of young green turtles are carnivorous. The adults, on the other hand, are herbivores and exclusively consume marine plants like sea grass and marine algae. Today, green turtles are categorized as a "Endangered" species.

Olive Ridley Turtle

Of all the sea turtles, the Olive Ridley is the smallest. The adults can reach a length of 65 cm and weigh less than 40 kg. The tropical Indian, south Atlantic, and Pacific Oceans are where they are most commonly found. They consume fish, crustaceans, and some marine plants since they are omnivore. Currently, Olive Ridley turtles are classified as a ''Endangered'' species. Olive Ridley turtles lay their eggs on a beach in a large group known as a "Arribada" in a few nations. The arrival is the meaning of this Spanish term. For instance, during the course of a few weeks, 600,000 Olive Ridley turtles have been observed breeding in India on the same beach. Depend upon the safety of a select few significant beaches for nesting.

Leatherback Turtle

Of all the sea turtles, the leatherback is the biggest. They typically weigh around 600 kg and can grow to lengths of more over 2 meters. The biggest Leatherback ever discovered was 918 kg huge! They have a dorsal black color that predominates, with varying amounts of white or lighter dotting. On the neck, spots may be pinkish. In quest of their meal, leatherbacks would travel great distances. They only consume jellyfish. The Leatherback turtle can live in the severe cold because, unlike other turtles, it can control its own body temperature because layers of fatty tissue insulate its body. They have been spotted feasting on jellyfish in the seas of the Arctic Circle. to a depth of 1500 meters to look for deep-sea jellyfish. The Leatherback's body experiences extreme water pressure at these depths, yet because of its flexible shell, which resists breaking, the turtle may safely eat. The status of leatherback turtles is now listed as "Critically Endangered."

Hawksbill Turtle

The adults of hawksbill turtles, which typically weigh around 60 kg and reach lengths of up to 90 cm, are likewise rather tiny. They are largely carnivorous and live in tropical coastal waters near coral reefs. They eat a wide range of creatures, including as jellyfish, sponges, and crabs. The English term "hawksbill turtle" refers to the creature's tiny, bird-like beak, which it employs to snare prey that is lurking in small cracks. Hawksbill turtles may consume poisonous sponges. Hawksbills may really store the poisons in their own body rather than getting poisoned. A human who consumes Hawksbill turtle flesh runs the risk of developing severe food illness. Due to centuries of killing of Hawksbill turtles for their shells by people all over the world, their numbers are now critically low. The shell is used to create "tortoiseshell" decorations once it has been cleaned and polished. Hawksbill turtles are currently categorized as being "Critically Endangered."

Loggerhead Turtle

Loggerhead turtles may weigh up to 180 kg and reach lengths of 1 meter. This species builds its eggs on beaches in both tropical and temperate regions, including the Mediterranean and the South East coast of the United States. Despite being one of the most widespread species in the globe, this species is the least prevalent nesting species in Sri Lanka. It is crucial to take prompt conservation action to safeguard the Loggerhead turtles in Sri Lanka since they are indigenous to the Indian subcontinent, only lay their eggs there, and have distinctive coloration that suggests a distinct population with unique DNA. Loggerhead turtles eat mostly crustaceans and mollusks since they are carnivorous. The term "Loggerhead" refers to the enormous head that houses a powerful pair of jaws that are perfect for crushing crustaceans and mollusks. The status of loggerhead turtles was changed to "Endangered."

Turtle Hatchery Sri Lanka

The turtle hatcheries of Sri Lanka are vital conservation centers that play a crucial role in protecting and preserving the country's endangered turtle species. These hatcheries provide a safe and controlled environment for sea turtles to lay their eggs and ensure the survival of their offspring.
